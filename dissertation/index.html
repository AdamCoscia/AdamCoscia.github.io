Abstract

The advent of powerful new large language models (LLMs) has catalyzed a surge in LLM-powered educational technologies, enabling transformational advances that can empower learner agency, deliver personalized study materials, and promote active learning. Data scientists in education, called learning engineers, are at the forefront of this technological revolution, leading efforts to integrate LLMs into adaptive learning experiences. Yet persistent pedagogical risks, from bias and hallucinations to unfair grading and misalignment with instructional goals, highlight a critical gap. Existing tools for selecting, fine-tuning, and evaluating LLMs are not designed to address the unique challenges of educational contexts, limiting the broader adoption of LLM-powered tools for learning.


To address this gap, this thesis explores how human-in-the-loop visual analytics can equip learning engineers to better discover, understand, and address these risks by combining automated analysis with interactive visualizations that support more informed, iterative decision-making throughout the LLM development lifecycle. Specifically, this thesis makes contributions to visual analytics, human computer interaction, artificial intelligence, and educational technology across several related thrusts.


1. Uncovering Harmful LLM Biases and Stereotypes

🧠 KnowledgeVIS enables visual exploration of latent biases in foundation LLMs during model selection.

2. Diagnosing LLM Decision-Making Criteria

📊 iScore visualizes internal and external model perturbations to diagnosis unintended decision-making during fine-tuning.

3. Measuring and Visualizing LLM Trustworthiness

🤝 Our novel trustworthiness framework makes pedagogical risks interpretable and traceable during LLM evaluation.

4. Broadening Access to Visual Analytics

🚀 TrustyVis implements modular visual analytics components for reusable integration into real-world workflows.

Professional Bio (May 2026)

Adam Coscia is a PhD Candidate in Human-Centered Computing at Georgia Institute of Technology, where he develops AI-augmented visual analytics systems that advance explainable, adaptive, and collaborative human-AI decision-making. Through rich mixed-methods studies and real-world deployments, he investigates how interactive visualizations can improve experts' ability to calibrate trust in AI, incorporate AI-driven insight discovery, and achieve timely, defensible decision-making with agentic AI partners.


His interdisciplinary research spans visual analytics, human-centered computing, and artificial intelligence, with applications across national security, robotics, education, and space systems. His systems have been deployed to help researchers, scientists, analysts, and engineers at NASA JPL, MIT Lincoln Lab, Caltech, Vanderbilt, and the DOW. His work has led to funding and sponsorships from the NSF, DARPA, NASA, and NSA, as well as publications at premier HCI and visualization venues including CHI, VIS, TVCG, UIST, and IUI.
